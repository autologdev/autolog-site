How to Keep on Top of Car Maintenance in the UK

Guides for UK drivers · 8 min read

Keeping on top of car maintenance means tracking service due dates, MOT expiry, and mileage-based tasks so that nothing is missed or deferred past the point at which it causes damage or fails a legal check. It requires a combination of knowing what your vehicle needs, when it needs it, and having a system to ensure you act in time.

Why Staying on Top of Maintenance Matters

The consequences of missed or deferred maintenance fall into two categories: legal and mechanical.

On the legal side, driving without a valid MOT is a criminal offence, and lapsed road tax can result in automatic fines. These are administrative failures that occur not from neglect of the vehicle itself but from losing track of renewal dates.

On the mechanical side, missed service intervals lead to accelerated wear. Engines running on degraded oil sustain more wear per mile. Brake pads left beyond their service life damage discs. Tyres run below the legal tread depth of 1.6mm become both illegal and unsafe. Many of these failures are cumulative — the vehicle does not break down immediately, but reliability and resale value decline progressively.

Understanding Your Vehicle's Maintenance Schedule

Every vehicle has a manufacturer-specified maintenance schedule, typically found in the owner's manual. This schedule defines when key tasks should be carried out, expressed in either time intervals or mileage — whichever comes first.

Common maintenance tasks and their typical triggers include:

  • Engine oil and filter change: Every 10,000–12,000 miles or 12 months, depending on the vehicle. Some modern vehicles with variable service intervals may extend this.
  • Air filter replacement: Typically every 15,000–20,000 miles or 2 years.
  • Brake fluid change: Every 2 years, regardless of mileage — brake fluid absorbs moisture over time.
  • Spark plugs (petrol engines): Varies significantly by type — conventional plugs every 30,000–40,000 miles; iridium plugs may last up to 100,000 miles.
  • Timing belt (cam belt): Typically every 60,000–80,000 miles or 5–6 years. Failure to replace on schedule can result in catastrophic engine damage.
  • Tyre rotation: Every 6,000–8,000 miles, where applicable.
  • Coolant change: Every 2–5 years depending on the manufacturer's specification.

These intervals are guides — the owner's manual for a specific vehicle is always the authoritative source.

The Key Dates to Track

Beyond the service schedule, there are fixed-date obligations that every UK driver must monitor:

  • MOT expiry: Applies to most vehicles over three years old. Missing the expiry date means driving illegally, which also invalidates motor insurance in most cases.
  • Vehicle Excise Duty (road tax): Renews monthly or annually. The DVLA sends a reminder, but these can be missed. The vehicle's tax status can be checked at any time via the DVLA's vehicle enquiry service.
  • Insurance renewal: Annual policies require active renewal. Auto-renewal is common but worth reviewing — premiums often increase significantly at renewal unless the policy is actively compared.
  • Breakdown cover renewal: Where applicable.

Using Mileage to Plan Maintenance

Mileage is a more reliable trigger for many maintenance tasks than elapsed time, particularly for drivers with above or below average annual mileage. A driver covering 20,000 miles per year will need an oil change every six months; a driver covering 5,000 miles per year will need one annually but may need brake fluid changed on a time basis before the mileage trigger is reached.

Recording the odometer reading at each service — and logging current mileage periodically — allows a driver to estimate accurately how far away the next service milestone is at any given point. This is particularly important for the timing belt, where the consequences of exceeding the service interval are most severe.

How Maintenance Is Typically Tracked

Most drivers rely on one or more of the following:

  • Garage reminders: Many garages send a service reminder by post or text when they calculate the next service is approaching. This is useful but depends on the garage having current contact details and the driver continuing to use the same garage.
  • Calendar reminders: Setting annual reminders for MOT and tax renewal is a minimal but effective approach for those two tasks.
  • Paper service booklet: Records past services but provides no forward reminders.
  • Spreadsheets: Can be structured to calculate next service dates from entered mileage data, but require consistent updating.

A More Reliable System

The limitation shared by all informal methods is that they require the driver to maintain and consult them consistently. A system that surfaces the right information at the right time — without the driver needing to seek it out — is significantly more likely to result in timely action.

Vehicle management apps such as AutoLog address this by pulling MOT and tax expiry dates from DVLA data automatically, allowing drivers to log service entries against actual mileage, and maintaining a structured record of all maintenance carried out. When mileage is updated, the app can indicate proximity to the next service interval — removing the need for drivers to perform their own calculations from a spreadsheet or service booklet.

Maintenance-Based vs. Time-Based Thinking

Drivers who think about maintenance primarily in terms of annual events — the service, the MOT, the insurance renewal — will miss mileage-triggered tasks if their driving patterns are irregular. Combining both approaches — tracking both dates and mileage — gives a complete picture of what a vehicle needs and when.

This is particularly relevant for vehicles used heavily for a period and then lightly — for example, a car used intensively for a work project and then driven less. The mileage accrued during the intensive period may trigger a service need that a purely time-based approach would miss for months.

Key Takeaways

  • Staying on top of car maintenance requires tracking both date-based obligations (MOT, tax, insurance) and mileage-based service tasks
  • The manufacturer's service schedule — in the owner's manual — defines what your specific vehicle needs and when
  • The timing belt is the most critical mileage-based task — exceeding the interval can cause severe engine damage
  • MOT expiry is a legal obligation; driving without a valid MOT also typically invalidates insurance
  • Informal systems (garage reminders, calendars) work for fixed dates but are unreliable for mileage-based tasks
  • Logging actual mileage against a maintenance record allows accurate calculation of when the next service milestone will be reached
