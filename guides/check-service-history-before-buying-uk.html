How to Check a Car's Service History Before Buying in the UK

Guides for UK drivers · 8 min read

Checking a car's service history before buying involves verifying that documented maintenance has been carried out at the correct intervals, cross-referencing records for consistency, and using official sources such as MOT history to identify any gaps or discrepancies. It is one of the most reliable indicators of how a used vehicle has been cared for.

Why Service History Matters When Buying a Used Car

A used car's service history provides a traceable record of how it has been maintained throughout its life. Regular servicing at the manufacturer's recommended intervals reduces wear, extends component life, and helps avoid unexpected failures.

For a buyer, the practical significance is straightforward: a vehicle with a full and verifiable service history is demonstrably lower risk than one without. It is also typically worth more, meaning that checking the history carefully gives buyers leverage when negotiating on price for vehicles with incomplete records.

What Documents to Request

When viewing a used vehicle in the UK, request the following before committing to a purchase:

  • Service booklet (paper or digital): A stamped service booklet — manufacturer-issued or independent — provides the primary record of scheduled maintenance. Check each entry for date, mileage, work carried out, and the stamp or signature of the garage.
  • Service invoices: Invoices from garages are arguably more informative than a booklet stamp alone. They detail exactly what was done, what parts were used, and what was charged. Request all available invoices, not just those from the most recent service.
  • V5C logbook: Confirm that the seller's name matches the registered keeper on the V5C, and that the vehicle identification number (VIN) matches the car.
  • MOT certificates: Previous MOT certificates provide independent confirmation of mileage at each annual test and any advisories or failures recorded at the time.

How to Check MOT History

MOT history is publicly accessible for free using the vehicle's registration number via the DVSA's official service at check-mot.service.gov.uk. This check should be carried out for every used vehicle under serious consideration.

MOT history reveals:

  • Mileage recorded at each annual test — allowing you to verify the current odometer reading is plausible
  • Pass and fail history — repeated failures on the same issue suggest unresolved faults
  • Advisory notices over time — a trend of worsening advisories may indicate deferred maintenance
  • Gaps in the record — years without an MOT may indicate a SORN period, or may warrant further investigation

Cross-Referencing Mileage

Mileage consistency is one of the most important things to verify. Compare:

  • The mileage at each MOT test with the current odometer reading — the progression should be logical and broadly consistent year on year
  • Mileage on service invoices with MOT records from the same period
  • The seller's stated annual mileage with the overall odometer reading and the vehicle's age

Any unexplained reduction in recorded mileage is a serious concern and should prompt a full vehicle history check from a reputable provider, which includes a clocking indicator.

Verifying Dealership Records

If a vehicle has been serviced at a franchised dealership, the service record may be held electronically within that manufacturer's network. Contacting the relevant dealer with the vehicle's VIN can often confirm whether services were completed — even if the physical booklet has been lost. This is particularly useful for main dealer-serviced vehicles from manufacturers such as Ford, Volkswagen, or BMW.

Independent garage services are harder to verify remotely, making the original invoices more important.

What Incomplete Service History Means

Incomplete service history does not automatically rule out a purchase, but it does shift the risk profile:

  • It may indicate services were carried out but not documented — common with DIY maintenance or informal garage work
  • It may indicate services were skipped — particularly relevant for components with time-sensitive replacement intervals, such as timing belts
  • It creates uncertainty that justifies either a lower purchase price or an independent inspection before committing

On high-mileage vehicles, the absence of a timing belt service record at the mileage where it would normally be due is a specific concern. Replacement costs can be significant, and failure can cause major engine damage.

Sharing Service History as a Seller

For sellers, presenting a complete and well-organised service history significantly strengthens buyer confidence and supports a higher asking price. Digital records allow sellers to share documentation easily — a structured record covering service history, documents, and costs can be shared with a prospective buyer without handing over physical documents.

Apps such as AutoLog allow sellers to share a vehicle's documented history with a prospective buyer directly, providing a clear and verifiable record alongside the physical documents.

Key Takeaways

  • Always check a used car's service history before buying — it directly reflects how the vehicle has been maintained
  • Request the service booklet, service invoices, V5C, and MOT certificates
  • Check MOT history free at check-mot.service.gov.uk using the registration number
  • Verify mileage consistency across MOT records, service invoices, and the current odometer
  • Franchised dealer service records can often be verified by contacting the relevant dealer network
  • Incomplete service history warrants either a lower price or an independent pre-purchase inspection
