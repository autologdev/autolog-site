What Records Should You Keep for Your Car in the UK?

Guides for UK drivers · 7 min read

UK drivers should retain records across several categories: legal compliance documents (MOT certificate, insurance, road tax confirmation), ownership documents (V5C logbook), maintenance records (service history, receipts), and financial records (running costs, mileage logs). The complete set protects owners in disputes, supports resale value, and satisfies legal obligations.

Why Keeping Car Records Matters

Many drivers keep only the documents required at the roadside — insurance and an MOT certificate — and discard or misplace everything else. This approach creates problems that may not be apparent until a dispute, sale, or insurance claim arises.

A complete set of vehicle records serves three practical functions: it demonstrates that a vehicle has been legally maintained, it provides evidence in the event of a disagreement, and it supports the vehicle's value at resale.

Legal Compliance Documents

These are the documents that confirm your vehicle meets UK legal requirements. While some can be verified electronically, physical or digital copies should be retained.

  • MOT certificate: Issued each year the vehicle passes its MOT test. Required for most vehicles over three years old. The certificate number and expiry date should be recorded.
  • Motor insurance certificate: Proof of at least third-party insurance. A physical certificate should be kept even if your insurer provides electronic confirmation.
  • Vehicle Excise Duty (road tax) confirmation: Road tax is now registered electronically, but confirmation emails or renewal reminders serve as useful records.

Ownership Documents

  • V5C logbook (vehicle registration certificate): The V5C is the primary ownership document for a UK-registered vehicle. It records the registered keeper's details, vehicle specification, and registration history. It should be updated promptly following any change of address or ownership transfer.
  • Purchase documentation: The original bill of sale, receipt, or sales contract from when the vehicle was acquired. This is important for insurance purposes and for establishing chain of ownership.
  • Finance agreement (if applicable): If the vehicle was purchased on finance, the agreement details — settlement amounts, outstanding balance, and lender contact details — should be retained.

Maintenance and Service Records

This category is where many drivers are least organised, but it has the most direct impact on resale value and mechanical reliability.

  • Service history: Records of all routine servicing, including dates, mileage, work carried out, and the name of the garage. Whether recorded in a stamped booklet or digitally, continuity matters.
  • Repair invoices: All receipts for mechanical or bodywork repairs, whether carried out at a dealership or independent garage. These confirm what work has been done and at what cost.
  • Parts receipts: Receipts for parts purchased and fitted, particularly for significant components such as tyres, brake pads, batteries, or timing belts.
  • Recall completion notices: If a manufacturer recall has been carried out on the vehicle, confirmation of completion should be retained. Outstanding recalls can affect insurance and resale.
  • Warranty documentation: Terms, conditions, and any claim correspondence relating to manufacturer or extended warranties.

Financial Records

For personal budgeting, tax purposes, or business use, a record of vehicle expenditure is valuable.

  • Fuel log: A record of fuel fill-ups, including dates, volume, cost per litre, and mileage. This allows cost-per-mile calculations and fuel efficiency tracking over time.
  • Running cost log: A broader expense record covering insurance premiums, road tax, servicing, repairs, parking, and any other recurring costs.
  • Mileage log: For self-employed drivers or those using a vehicle for business purposes, a mileage log is required to support HMRC mileage claims.

How Records Are Typically Stored

The traditional approach is to keep all physical documents in a folder or wallet stored in the vehicle, with important paperwork also held at home. This works adequately for legal documents but is impractical for ongoing logs and receipts.

Spreadsheets offer more structure for financial records, but require manual input and provide no reminders or alerts. They are also not accessible on the move.

A More Practical Approach

Digital storage has made it considerably easier to maintain comprehensive vehicle records. Scanned documents, photographed receipts, and structured log entries can all be held in a single location that is accessible from a smartphone.

Apps such as AutoLog are designed specifically for this purpose — allowing UK drivers to store service records, scan documents, log fuel and costs, and track mileage against a specific vehicle, with MOT and tax status pulled automatically from DVLA data.

Key Takeaways

  • Keep all legal compliance documents: MOT certificate, insurance certificate, road tax confirmation
  • Retain ownership documents: V5C logbook, purchase receipt, and any finance agreement
  • Store maintenance records: service history, repair invoices, parts receipts, and recall notices
  • Log financial records: fuel costs, running expenses, and a mileage log if using the vehicle for business
  • Digital storage improves accessibility, reduces loss risk, and makes sharing records easier at point of sale
